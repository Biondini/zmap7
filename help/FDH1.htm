1   Introduction

 

Earthquake distributions are considered fractal, but indirectly. There are two possible models, which result in fractal earthquake distributions (Turcotte, 1997). The first model considers that a fractal distribution of faults leads to a fractal distribution of earthquakes, because each earthquake is characteristic of the fault on which it occurs. The second assumes that each fault has a fractal distribution of earthquakes. Turcotte’s (1997) observations agree with the first hypothesis. Fractal distributions imply that the number of objects larger than a specified size has a power-law dependence on the size. The fractal distributions are the only distributions that do not include a characteristic length scale, and therefore, are applicable to scale invariant phenomena. As earthquake distributions obey fractal statistics, the fractal dimension can characterize them.

The fractal dimension is a real number, which measures the geometry of a distribution and presumably varies as a function of time and space. To be precise the correlation dimension is calculated (Grassberger & Procaccia, 1983, Volant & Grasso, 1994). This is estimated with the slope method, which consists in estimating the slope of the double-logarithmic plot of the correlation integral versus the distance (Figure 1.1).

The suitability of the scaling range is verified with the slope test (figure 2.1, bottom right). This consists in calculating the first derivative between each two points of the correlation integral curve and plotting it versus the logarithmic distance. The scaling range is defined within the region where the slope is most constant. This technique enables to eliminate the depopulation and saturation effects fairly consistently.
